For a long time, dispatch is just something you do in your head.

You know the crews. You know the trucks. You know who is where. A whiteboard and a few notes are enough, and it works.

Until one day it doesn't. Here are the signs you have crossed that line.

## You're dispatching from memory

The first sign is that the whole picture lives in one person's head.

That person knows which crew is on which job, which truck is free, who is off sick. Which is fine, until they are out on a move themselves, or off for the day, and nobody else can answer a simple question about the schedule.

When the operation stops if one person is unreachable, you have outgrown the setup.

## The double-bookings have started

The clearest sign is overbooking.

Two jobs booked for the same truck. The same crew promised to two customers. It happened once and you caught it. Then it happened again. Now you check and re-check before confirming anything, because you have learned not to trust the whiteboard.

Double-bookings are not bad luck. They are what happens when the schedule lives in too many places and no single place shows the truth.

## You can't see what's free

Ask yourself a simple question: right now, which crews and trucks are available next Thursday?

If answering means calling someone, checking a whiteboard, and crossing your fingers, that is the problem. You cannot plan well when you cannot see clearly. Every booking becomes a small gamble.

## You're gambling on every job

That is really what manual dispatch becomes as you grow. A series of small bets.

Betting the truck is free. Betting the crew remembers. Betting nothing was double-booked. Most of the time you win. But the times you lose cost you a scramble, an angry customer, or a job you couldn't actually run.

## What changes with a real system

A dispatch system does one core thing: it shows you the truth in one place.

Which crews and trucks are free, at a glance. A booking that can't clash with one already there. A schedule anyone on the team can see, not just the person holding it in their head.

The gambling stops. You plan from what is actually true, not what you hope is true.
